Feature Article · Beauty & Digital Media April 2026 · Sociolla / Beauty Journal

Learning K-Beauty Should Feel Like Talking
to Your Most Knowledgeable Friend

"In the K-beauty content space, the winner isn't whoever posts the most — it's whoever earns the most trust. Beauty Journal proved empathy beats authority every time."

This deep-dive feature examines how Beauty Journal by Sociolla cracked the code for Indonesian Gen Z beauty consumers — not by teaching harder, but by talking warmer. The piece coins and analyzes the platform's core content framework: "Bestie-Talk", a communication style that swaps instructional rigidity for organic, friend-to-friend language.


Where competitors explained glass skin in technical terms, Beauty Journal wrote: "Just tap with your fingers for a more natural look, like you just ate an ice lolly." One sentence containing technique, visual outcome, and instant relatability. That's not a writing accident — that's editorial intelligence backed by consumer psychology research on beginner anxiety.


The piece also breaks down the platform's most strategically valuable innovation: cloud skin — a localized reinterpretation of glass skin that swaps heavy highlighter layers for a dewy-matte finish suited to Indonesia's humid, high-heat climate. This is what separates genuine cultural adaptation from surface-level translation.

Core Argument
In beauty content, empathy is a bigger competitive moat than production value. Trust compounds; ad budgets don't.

Opinion & Cultural Essay · Education / Digital Media April 2026 · Original Academic Research, UNY

The Library Nobody Opens: When Universities
Silence Their Own Generation

"Student academic work is objectified cultural capital — high-value intellectual output left to decay not because of limited ideas, but because of limited systems."

This essay argues one of the most uncomfortable truths in Indonesian higher education: every semester, tens of millions of documents — essays on poverty, poems about inequality, analyses of vanishing local cultures — are created and destroyed inside a bureaucratic loop that never touches public life. The piece reframes this not as academic waste, but as a cultural crisis we've normalized.


The argument hinges on a powerful paradox: Generation Z is the most digitally fluent generation in human history, yet the deeper they go into higher education, the more thoroughly the system seals them into what the essay calls a "soundproof room" — assignments written for one person, graded by one person, and then permanently silenced. Risyad challenges the "Strawberry Generation" label not by defending Gen Z, but by asking who created the fragility.


The piece draws on real cases — a literature student's poem about migrant wages reaching 1.4 million views, an anthropology thread about dying regional languages cited in parliament — as proof that the format of narrative-digital-advocacy content has power no 200-page policy report possesses. It closes with a concrete proposal rooted in international "renewable assignment" models, asking campuses to choose: prison or stage?

Core Argument
Academic systems produce cultural capital, then destroy it. Digital platforms offer an exit — but only if institutions are brave enough to use them.

How I Work

Three moves.
Every single time.

01
Listen Before Writing
I don't start with sentences. I start with research — the audience's fears, their vocabulary, their trust triggers. Copy written without this is just noise.
02
Anchor in Specifics
Vague claims die. Specific details stick. "86% of Gen Z relies on TikTok for beauty info" is more persuasive than "young people love social media." I work in specifics.
03
Write for the Human, Not the Algorithm
Algorithms change. Human psychology doesn't. I write for what makes people lean forward, not what makes dashboards tick — and the best copy does both.
